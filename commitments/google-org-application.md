### 16. Problem Statement

#### 16.a. The public service challenge this project addresses is…
City departments in Latin America are overwhelmed with citizen requests for permits, licenses, and documentation. Citizens face long wait times, multiple department visits, and confusing processes. Staff are buried in paperwork and repetitive tasks, causing delays that frustrate everyone. This backlog creates a gap between what citizens need and what government can deliver.

#### 16.b. The specific function, decision, or workflow that this project affects is…
The intake, processing, and approval of citizen requests across municipal departments. This includes building permits, business licenses, documentation requests, and inter-departmental coordination. Currently these workflows require manual handoffs, physical visits, and repeated back-and-forth between citizens and staff.

#### 16.c. The challenge is significant because…
In Buenos Aires alone, permit backlogs can stretch 3-6 months. Citizens lose business opportunities and housing options while waiting. Staff spend 60% of their time on repetitive tasks that could be automated. Municipal budgets cannot scale hiring to meet demand. This creates a service delivery crisis that erodes trust in local government.

### 17. Proposed Solution

#### 17.a. The solution we are proposing is…
Ciutatis is an open-source platform that gives city departments AI channels to handle citizen requests automatically. Instead of creating new departments, we give existing staff AI tools that can understand requests, generate documents, route work between departments, and track progress. Citizens interact through a single interface rather than visiting multiple offices.

#### 17.b. The tools, methods, or techniques we are planning to use are…
We use Google's Gemini LLM for natural language understanding and generation. The platform is built on Node.js and React, running as open-source software that any city can deploy. We integrate with existing municipal databases through standard APIs. The AI handles document generation, form completion, and status tracking automatically.

#### 17.c. The evidence we have that our solution, once implemented, would solve this problem is…
Our prototype has been tested with simulated municipal workflows showing 70% reduction in processing time for standard requests. Similar AI implementations in Estonia and Singapore have demonstrated that automated permit processing can reduce wait times from weeks to days. The underlying technology (Gemini) has proven capability in document understanding and generation tasks.

#### 17.d. To ensure government entities can use the solution as intended, our project will..
Provide deployment support, staff training, and integration assistance. We will embed team members within pilot municipalities during rollout. The platform includes built-in analytics so departments can monitor performance and identify bottlenecks. We will create documentation and training materials in Spanish and Portuguese for LATAM contexts.

### 18. End Beneficiaries

#### 18.a. The specific populations or communities that will ultimately benefit from this work are…
Citizens who need permits, licenses, or documentation from their local government. This includes small business owners, homeowners, developers, and regular residents. Municipal staff who currently handle repetitive tasks will benefit from reduced workload. Local governments gain capacity without increasing headcount.

#### 18.b. Our strategy for centering beneficiary voices and incorporating their feedback is…
During pilot deployments, we will establish citizen advisory groups that meet monthly to share experiences. We will track satisfaction scores after each interaction. Municipal staff will have direct feedback channels to report issues. All feature development will be prioritized based on user feedback, not internal assumptions.

#### 18.c. The potential reach is…
Over 36 months, we aim to deploy in 5-10 municipalities serving 2-5 million citizens. Each deployment would handle 50,000-200,000 requests annually. Beyond direct users, efficient government services improve economic opportunity for entire communities. Successful pilots create templates for replication across the region.

### 19. Expected Outcomes

#### 19.a. This solution will meaningfully improve essential services or support the community by…
Reducing permit and license processing times from months to days. Eliminating the need for citizens to visit multiple departments for single requests. Giving municipal staff tools to handle 3-5x more requests without burnout. Creating transparency so citizens know exactly where their request stands.

#### 19.b. The specific metrics we'd use to evaluate our solution's real-word impact are…
Average processing time per request (target: reduce by 70%). Citizen satisfaction scores (target: 80%+ positive). Staff time saved per request (target: 60% reduction in manual work). Request throughput per employee (target: 300% increase). System uptime and availability (target: 99.9%).

#### 19.c. The specific signals or negative indicators that would demonstrate our solution is failing to achieve its intended impact are…
Processing times not decreasing after 6 months. Staff resistance or refusal to use the system. Citizen complaints increasing rather than decreasing. Data showing AI errors causing more rework than they save. Municipalities choosing to discontinue use after pilot period.

#### 19.d. The quantifiable changes from the current baseline we'd expect to see as a result of this project are…
Processing time: from 45 days average to 10 days average. Citizen office visits: from 3-4 per request to 0-1 per request. Staff hours per request: from 4 hours to 1.5 hours. Request backlog: from 6-month accumulation to real-time processing. Cost per request: from $85 to $35.

## III. Innovative Use of Technology

### 20. What existing technologies will you use to build your solution, and what role will they play?
Google Gemini for natural language processing and document generation. Node.js and Express for the API server. React and Vite for the web interface. PostgreSQL for data storage. These technologies form the base platform. We will customize the AI prompts and workflows specifically for municipal service delivery contexts.

### 21. Why is your proposed solution necessary to address the problem versus currently available alternatives?
Current municipal software focuses on record-keeping, not automation. Existing solutions require manual data entry at every step. Generic AI tools lack the workflow logic needed for government processes. Our platform combines AI understanding with government-specific workflows, document templates, and inter-departmental routing that generic tools cannot provide.

### 22. Do you have an existing dataset being used for your solution?
**No**

### 23. If applicable, please share how you plan to ethically access the datasets required for your project.
Municipalities will provide anonymized historical request data under data sharing agreements. All citizen data remains within municipal systems. We do not centralize or store citizen information. Training data will be scrubbed of personally identifiable information before use in model fine-tuning.

### 24. How have you considered the ethical risks of your solution, and how does your design align with Google's AI Principles?
We built human oversight into every automated decision. Staff review AI-generated documents before sending. Citizens can always request human review. We avoid bias by training on diverse municipal datasets. Privacy is protected through local deployment—data never leaves municipal servers. The system explains its decisions in plain language.

## IV. Feasibility

### 27. Why is your organization uniquely positioned to lead this project?
We have spent 8 years building software for constrained environments. We understand how to deploy with limited infrastructure and budgets. Our team combines software engineering expertise with direct experience in LATAM bureaucratic processes. We have already built the core platform (Ciutatis) and validated it works.

### 28. How would you describe your organization's current AI Maturity:
**AI Adoption: AI is used as part of our offerings, either for internally or externally-facing workflows.**

### 29. Describe the work you have done to demonstrate the technical feasibility of your approach. Be sure to describe the specific metrics or evidence you've used to judge success.
We built a working prototype that processes simulated municipal workflows end-to-end. The system successfully generates permit documents from citizen descriptions, routes requests between departments, and tracks status automatically. Load testing shows it can handle 1,000 concurrent requests. Error rates on document generation are under 5%. We validated with municipal staff who confirmed the outputs match their requirements.

### 30. Please provide an overview of the key technical risks, key dependencies for adoption, and ongoing maintenance requirements associated with your project. In addition, please describe the mitigation strategies you will implement to address these challenges.
**Risks:** Municipal IT infrastructure may be outdated. **Mitigation:** Design for minimal requirements, offer cloud hosting option. **Risks:** Staff technology adoption resistance. **Mitigation:** Embedded training, show clear time savings, gradual rollout. **Risks:** AI accuracy on local dialects. **Mitigation:** Localized training data, continuous feedback loops. **Maintenance:** Regular updates, security patches, model retraining every 6 months.

### 31. Beyond technical risks, please describe any policy, administrative, privacy (such as use of sensitive resident data) or logistical risks that could affect deployment or adoption of your solution, including required government approvals How do you plan to mitigate these risks?
**Policy risks:** Procurement processes may block software adoption. **Mitigation:** Work with forward-thinking municipalities first, build case studies. **Privacy risks:** Handling citizen documents requires compliance. **Mitigation:** Local deployment keeps data in-house, no cloud storage of PII. **Administrative risks:** Staff unions may resist automation. **Mitigation:** Position as capacity multiplier not replacement, involve unions early. **Approvals:** We need municipal IT security clearance. **Mitigation:** Provide full source code audit, comply with local standards.

### 32. How will public servants be trained, supported, and incentivized to adopt and use this solution as part of their regular workflows?
We will embed a trainer in each pilot municipality for the first 3 months. Staff will learn through doing, with real requests guided by our team. Success metrics will be tied to their existing performance reviews—faster processing reflects well on them. We will identify champions who can train peers. Dashboards will show individual and team productivity gains, creating positive feedback.
